# Composition - Editorial Outline

Distill the raw source material, brief, and candidate visuals into the editorial outline of a composition: what it covers, what each piece is there to communicate, the sequence, the single point, and the feeling.

Use after the strategic brief, knowledge, media, and references for a composition have been gathered and the job is to decide what the page is actually about before any structural or copy decisions are made.

Trigger for turning a pile of gathered material into an editorial position: deciding what matters, what doesn't make the cut, finding the single point, and naming the feeling the composition should produce.

What this prevents is jumping straight to layout or copy while the raw material is still unranked, or building a page that covers everything equally and therefore says nothing.

Do not use for compositions where the outline has already been done and confirmed. Do not use in place of the brief itself. The brief says what we're making and why. This says what the thing we're making is actually about once we've looked at the material.

---

## Inputs you're working from

Not every composition arrives with the same set of inputs. The strategic brief is always present. The rest depends on how far the project has been developed. Read everything you're given before doing anything, and understand what each module is so you know what's available and what's missing.

**Strategic brief** (always present). Names the subject, the coverage goals, any design motifs or graphic ideas already committed to, and the types of imagery planned. This is the frame. Everything else serves it.

**Knowledge module** (when present). The raw source material: facts, quotes, specs, history, context, narrative fragments, whatever has been gathered about the subject. This is the substance the outline is built from. If there is no knowledge module, the brief and whatever other modules are present are your only source material, and the outline will be thinner. Flag that.

**Media module** (when present). Candidate images and video, with any context directives noting what each one shows or why it was selected. When media is available, it constrains the outline: a piece of the story that has strong corresponding imagery is stronger than one that has to be carried entirely by words, and that matters when you're ranking.

**References module** (when present). Layout and composition references, with any context directives noting what each one demonstrates. References inform the composition direction step more than the outline, but if they reveal structural expectations or constraints that affect what should be covered, account for that.

**Identity module** (when present). Full guidelines for the graphic language of the project or brand: typography, color, layout principles, graphic devices, visual tone. This is not source material for the outline's content, but it tells you what the composition's visual vocabulary is. When the identity module is present, the outline's notes on visual proof should account for what the identity system can and can't carry.

**Component module** (when present). Final or near-final designed elements: HTML sections, UI components, built page pieces. These are not references or inspiration. They are committed design decisions that already exist. When a component module is present, parts of the composition's structure are already locked. The outline needs to account for what those components cover and what they don't, because the outline's job is still to get the full editorial position right, and existing components may have gaps or may cover things the brief didn't anticipate.

Read everything you're given. Note what's present and what's not. The modules you have shape what the outline can do. A composition with a brief, full knowledge, media, identity, and components is a different job than a composition with just a brief and some raw notes. The outline should reflect the depth of what's available, and flag where missing modules leave gaps.

## Reconcile before you outline

Before writing the outline, reconcile the brief's coverage goals against the knowledge.

The brief says to cover certain things. The knowledge contains what it contains. These two lists will not match perfectly. Name what maps cleanly, what the brief asks for that the knowledge doesn't support well, and what the knowledge contains that the brief didn't anticipate. This reconciliation is not the tensions section at the end. It's the prerequisite. You need to see the full landscape of what's available and what's missing before you decide what the composition actually covers and in what order.

---

## What distillation actually is

You are writing the editorial outline of the composition. A full, ordered account of everything the page will cover, stated completely enough that someone reading it understands what each part is, why it's there, and what it's supposed to make the audience think or feel.

Each entry in the outline is a complete thought. It might be one sentence or it might be a short paragraph. It should say:

**What this part of the composition actually covers**, with the real specifics from the source material. Not a label like "materials" or "program." The actual substance: what the materials are, what's notable about them, what they do, how they relate to the larger story. Stay as close to the source language and detail as the material warrants. If the source material is rich, the entry should be rich. If it's thin, say so.

**What that coverage is there to communicate.** Not a tagline. The real reason it matters to this composition and its audience. Why this part earns space on the page. What understanding or feeling it builds that the parts before it set up and the parts after it depend on.

**What visual proof exists for it in the media candidates, or doesn't.** A part of the story that has strong corresponding imagery operates differently in a composition than one that has to be carried entirely by words. Name the connection or name the gap.

The entries are in the order the audience needs to encounter them for the whole thing to build correctly. This is editorial sequencing: what has to land first to make the rest make sense, what builds on what, what the composition opens with and what it closes with. It is not a page layout. "You need to understand X before Y hits right" is a sequencing decision. "X is above the fold" is a structural decision that belongs in the next step.

Do not flatten the source material to make it fit a format. If a piece of the story is complicated, let it be complicated in the outline. The composition direction step that follows will figure out how to structure it. The outline's job is to get it right, not to get it tidy.

The outline should read like editorial direction, not draft copy. You are describing what the composition covers and why, not writing how it will say it. If the language starts sounding like prose that's trying to be good, pull back. This is a planning document.

---

## The single point

After the full outline, state the single point the entire thing ladders up to. One to two sentences. If the page could only land one thing, this is it. It should be specific to this subject, not a category-level statement. "This is a good project" is not a point. "This project proves that [specific claim about what it did, how it did it, or why it matters] is possible" is closer. The single point should be clearly traceable back through the outline entries.

---

## The feeling

The emotional register the composition should produce. Not a mood board and not a single word. A short, honest description of what someone should feel after taking it in, grounded in the subject itself. "Quiet confidence that this was done with unusual care and that the care is visible in the result" is a feeling. "Luxury" is a category. "Inspiring" is empty. The feeling should be something the source material actually supports, not something you're projecting onto it.

---

## Tensions and gaps

The material will not be clean. After the outline, surface anything unresolved:

Things the brief says to cover that the knowledge doesn't support well. Name what's missing.

Things the knowledge contains that are compelling but the brief didn't ask for. Recommend: include, cut, or hold for a different piece.

Parts of the story that are strong but have no visual proof in the media. They can still appear, but they'll work differently in the composition.

Parts of the story that compete for the opening or the climax. Don't resolve it by softening both into one blended statement. Present both, say which you'd lead with and why, and leave the decision open.

Do not smooth tensions out. The composition direction step needs to see them clearly.

---

## What to output

**The outline:** the full, ordered, comprehensive sequence of everything the composition covers. Each entry stated completely with its substance, its purpose, and its visual support or lack of it. In the order the audience needs to encounter them.

**Single point:** one to two sentences.

**Feeling:** short description, grounded in the subject.

**Tensions and gaps:** anything unresolved, missing, or in conflict. Recommendations where you have them.

Do not write copy. Do not suggest layout. Do not name sections or page units. Do not propose headlines. Those are the next step's job. The outline is the editorial position. It has to be right, and confirmed, before anything gets built on it.
