# Composition - Copywriting

Write the copy for a composition, unit by unit, into the structure and channel assignments the composition direction has already defined.

Use after the composition direction has been confirmed and the job is to write the actual words into their slots, sized to fit, serving what the visuals and graphic devices are already doing, not duplicating them.

Trigger for writing copy that knows what's next to it: what image is in the slot, what graphic device is carrying the data, what the through-line already embodies, and therefore what the words are uniquely responsible for.

What this prevents is copy written in a vacuum that only relays raw facts or inflates into filler, copy that restates what the image already shows, and copy that doesn't know its own size.

Do not use for ad headlines, taglines, SEO posts, or copy with no composition context. Do not use before the editorial outline and composition direction exist and are confirmed.

---

## Inputs you're working from

**Confirmed composition direction** (always present). The units in order, each with: what it does, the specific image, what the image carries, the graphic devices in play, the copy's job, what the copy must not do, and the unit's feeling. Also the through-line, the graphic and visual language devices, and any open decisions with their recommendations. This is your primary working document. Every word you write answers to it.

**Confirmed editorial outline** (always present). The full editorial position: what the composition covers, the substance and purpose of each part, the single point, the feeling. When you need to check whether the copy is landing the right substance or serving the right purpose, go back to the outline.

**Strategic brief** (always present). The frame. Coverage goals, design motifs, subject.

**Knowledge module** (when present). The raw source material you're writing from. The copy draws its specifics, facts, quotes, and details from here. Stay close to the source language where the source language is good. Do not paraphrase strong material into weaker versions of itself.

**Identity module** (when present). When it carries voice guidelines, tone, or verbal identity standards, those govern how the copy sounds. When it sets a type scale, that governs how much space each piece of copy gets. The identity's constraints are hard constraints.

**Component module** (when present). When built components already exist, the copy may need to fit specific containers, character limits, or structural patterns that the components define. Write to those constraints, not around them.

## How a composition is read

People decide whether to stay within a few seconds, off the first thing that hits them: image, big type, whatever opens. Body text is too slow to factor into that decision.

They scan before they read. The one line that survives a fast scan is the one that has to work. The rest is for the people who slow down.

Sequence builds belief, whether by scroll or by the eye moving across a composition. Each step answers what the last one raised and sets up the next.

A claim lands when its proof is next to it: the photo, the number, the quote. Not three sections away.

## What each channel carries

This is reference, not new work. The composition direction already made the specific assignments for each unit. This table is the underlying logic those assignments are built on. Use it to check your instincts while writing.


| Channel                                                     | Carries                                                      | Must not carry                                      |
| ----------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Photography / video**                                     | Place, mood, scale, texture, proof by showing                | Specifics, numbers, claims, the ask, logic          |
| **Big headline type** (seen before it's read)               | One idea, a hook, tone                                       | Anything past ~7 words, lists, nuance               |
| **Body text**                                               | Specifics, the argument, logic, sequence, voice              | First impressions — too slow to set mood            |
| **Small text** (eyebrows, captions, labels, buttons)        | Orientation, the next action, the thing the image can't show | The whole pitch                                     |
| **Graphic devices** (numbers, diagrams, icons, pull quotes) | Structure, comparison, emphasis, proof                       | Running narrative                                   |
| **Through-line** (the recurring idea across the whole)      | Continuity, identity, the organizing concept                 | Local specifics, anything that changes part to part |


Words carry specifics, names, numbers, claims, promises, logic, voice, and the ask.

Visuals carry place, mood, scale, and proof.

If a photo shows a quiet empty desert, don't write "quiet and remote." Write what the photo can't: the price, the catch, the invitation.

---

## Every piece works on three levels

What it shows. What it means. What it makes you feel.

The words line up with all three. A headline that shows the right information but hits the wrong feeling is wrong. A caption that means the right thing but shows the wrong detail is wrong. Check every piece against all three.

---

## Write to fit from the start

Every spot has a size set by its role and the design around it. When the composition direction names a type scale or the identity module sets one, that wins. When the component module defines containers, those win.

Write to the size the spot allows, not long-then-trimmed. Trimmed copy reads padded. Copy written to fit reads like it belongs.

The counts below are rough anchors for a conventional scroll page. For a different kind of composition, derive the equivalent for the spots it has. Two giant words floating in a motif have a count of two. A dense explanatory panel might take forty.


| Spot             | Words                                  |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Eyebrow / kicker | 1–4                                    |
| Lead headline    | 3–8                                    |
| Lead subhead     | 12–25                                  |
| Section headline | 2–6                                    |
| Body block       | 2–4 sentences, then break              |
| Caption          | 5–15, adds a fact the image can't show |
| Button           | 1–4, verb first, in the reader's words |
| Pull quote       | 8–20                                   |


If a message won't fit its spot, it's on the wrong channel. Move it. Don't cram it.

## Don't

Say what the image already shows.

Relay the source material as-is. That reads dry.

Over-narrativize it. That reads like filler.

Shape more than needed. Shape exactly enough to land the message and hit the feeling, no more.

Reach for autopilot words: curated, elevated, bespoke, nestled, discover, unlock, seamless, journey, experience, redefine, reimagine, oasis, escape, indulge.

Write a headline that would fit any company in the category. It has to carry one idea only this subject could.

Force copy bigger or longer than the spot allows. Wrong size means it's on the wrong channel. Move it.

Give every part the same density. A composition needs rhythm.

Repeat a headline in the caption next to it.

Explain the concept the through-line already embodies. The through-line is carrying that. The words do local work.

---

## What to output

Go unit by unit, in the order the composition direction defines them.

For each unit:

**Its job and the one thing that has to land.** Pulled from the composition direction. This is not new thinking. It's confirmation of what you're writing to.

**What the other channels are carrying.** Pulled from the composition direction. The image, the graphic devices, the through-line. Stated here so the copy visibly answers to it.

**The copy, sized to its spot.** Every piece: headlines, subheads, body, captions, labels, buttons, pull quotes, whatever the unit calls for. Written to the word counts and constraints the composition direction and identity set. Each piece doing only what the words are responsible for in this unit.

**The source material it draws on.** Which parts of the knowledge module or editorial outline this copy comes from. So the substance can be verified against the source.
