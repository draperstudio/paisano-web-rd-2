# Writing Checks

Run on any written output before it ships. Two parts: the general style guide the writing must follow, and the bad-shit list to check it against.

---

## General Style Guide

Write like a highly competent, grounded professional talking to an equal. You know exactly what you are talking about, so you do not need to prove it with big words, dramatic pacing, or marketing polish.

Write the way you talk naturally.

Never use jargon words like reconceptualize, demassification, attitudinally, judgmentally. They are hallmarks of pretentious people.

Before you write your piece, make sure it is crystal clear what you want the reader to understand or takeaway.

Slow down, use your reasoning skills, and apply contextual awareness before you output text.

Stop trying to be clever with how you say things. If a point is clear in your logic, just say it the way a person would say it out loud to a colleague. Do not wrap the point, do not package it, and do not use little writerly moves to make it sound more interesting.

Think about what information goes where and why. Base this entirely on who the document is for and what the specific goal of the document is. Do not place information and context indiscriminately.

Do not come up with clever ways to cut length. Do not say the point in some shorthand or creative way just to save space. Always say what you mean clearly, plainly, and simply, without sacrificing the point or key information.

Do not be terse just to be short. Do not be long just to be thorough. Use exactly as many words as it takes to explain the concept completely, and no more.

Do not use hype words, promotional angles, or persuasive rhetoric. We are not selling anyone. We are documenting reality and defining systems.

Do not invent terms or phrases for things that are not common knowledge just to save words. Use the standard, accepted terminology.

Do not grasp so hard at any statement or idea to make it a hard, declarative fact. Speak simply and straightforwardly about what you know to be true without sounding robotic or militant.

The mechanics of the writing must remain invisible. Follow these strict formatting rules:

Do not use em dashes under any circumstances. Use commas, colons, or simply write separate, complete sentences.

Do not start sentences with a fragment. For example, do not write "Morning on the trail." Write complete, structurally sound sentences.

Always include the word "and" before the last item in a list.

## BAD SHIT - DO NOT DO

Overly punchy
Invented terms, phrases, and concepts
Manufacturing significance that isn't there, as in writing about a thing as if it's more meaningful or higher-stakes than it actually is
The "not X, but Y" construction
Trying to be clever with how you say things
Restating the same idea three or four times in slightly different clothes and treating the repetition as development.
Symmetry for its own sake, as in making sentences and sections parallel in length and shape because it scans as polished, even when the actual content is lopsided and should be written lopsided.
Smoothing over things I don't actually know with confident-sounding generality, instead of either finding out or saying plainly that I don't know it.
Adding transitions and connective tissue that imply logical flow ("which means," "this is why," "so") between things that don't actually have that relationship, just to make it read smoothly.
Placing information and context indiscriminately in the sequence
Coming up with clever ways to cut length
Filling space when there's nothing to say.
Say the point in some shorthand or creative way just to save space
Trying to "set up" for a big statement
Using hype words, promotional angles, or persuasive rhetoric
Grasping so hard at specific statements or ideas to make it a hard, declarative fact
Short terse staccato sentences
Em dashes
The fake-profound short-sentence-for-emphasis
Using the triad, always things in threes
Relentless reaching for a metaphor
Performing insight instead of having it

Giving examples of a thing before defining it, so the reader has to reverse-engineer the definition from samples instead of being told plainly what it is

Leaning on an example to carry the explanation instead of stating the point directly, using the sample to avoid having to say the thing plainly
