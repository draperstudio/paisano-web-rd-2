# Controls - Graphic

Pulled from Notion (AD-CD-Controls-Logic > Controls > Controls - Graphic) on 7.11.26.

## Base (All Types)

### Upper Level

| Control | What to Address in Snippet Direction |
|---|---|
| **Technique** | The medium the graphic is made in and the look that comes with it. Whether it reads as made by a machine or by a hand, and how far it leans either way. How flat or dimensional the rendering is. The tool behind the marks and whether the viewer can tell what made them. How consistent the making is across the whole graphic. Era signals if the technique points to a particular time. |
| **Composition** | How the elements are arranged across the surface. The framework behind the arrangement, whether a strict grid or loose free placement, and how visible it is. Where the visual weight sits and whether the whole feels balanced or deliberately tipped. How much empty space there is and what it is doing. Whether one element is the clear focus or the eye spreads across the whole frame. The path the eye takes through the arrangement and what sets that order. |
| **Color** | Whether the graphic is in color or black and white. The palette, which colors are used and how many. How saturated or muted the colors are. Whether the palette runs warm or cold. How the colors relate to each other, whether they sit close together or push against each other. The balance of light and dark across the whole. Which color leads, which ones support, and which only show up as accents. |
| **Treatment** | What the graphic looks like as an artifact, separate from what it shows and from how it was made, since the making belongs to Technique. Grain, noise, or texture sitting on top of the work. Any print process and the marks it leaves behind. How crisp or rough the edges are. Whether the surface reads matte or shiny. Wear and age, whether the graphic looks fresh or handled and faded. Any material the surface feels like, paper, fabric, metal, screen. |
| **Mood** | The overall personality of the graphic as an image, how it feels before you register what it shows. The art direction intent for its tone, energy, and attitude, whether it reads serious or playful, quiet or loud, warm or cold. This is the target that the other upper-level controls should serve. |

### Lower Level

| Control | What to Address in the Snippet |
|---|---|
| **Subject** | What the graphic shows or stands for. How literal or abstract it is, whether the viewer names it at a glance or has to work for it. How specifically defined the subject is. Scale of the subject within the frame and how much room it takes. Number of elements and the relationships between them. Whether and how prominently people appear. |
| **Linework** | How the marks are drawn and filled. Whether the graphic is built from lines, solid shapes, or both, and which one carries it. The weight of the lines and whether it stays even or swells and thins. How clean or rough the marks are, ruled and exact or showing the hand. Whether the fills are flat or shaded and how they meet their edges. The drawing style as a decision of its own, the same style whatever the subject is. |
| **Form** | The shapes the graphic is built from, whether they are geometric or organic. How simple or complex the shapes are, how far they have been boiled down. Whether the edges are hard or soft and the corners sharp or rounded, and what that does to how serious or friendly the graphic feels. The proportions of the shapes and how they sit against each other. What the shapes are before any line style or detail, since how they are drawn belongs to Linework. |
| **Meaning** | What the graphic should say to the person looking at it. The idea behind the content, what the subject is there to communicate rather than what it literally is. Whether that idea should land at a glance or take a moment to get. How directly the graphic makes its point, said straight or implied. This is the target that the other lower-level controls should serve. |

## Specific Types

### Logo
*Mark, wordmark, monogram, lockup*

| Control | What to Address in the Snippet |
|---|---|
| **Mark Type** | What kind of mark it is. The name written out as a wordmark, initials as a monogram, a picture symbol, an abstract shape, or a symbol and name together. If there is a symbol, how literal or abstract it is and what it stands for. This is the first call to make, since it decides how the rest of the mark gets built. |
| **Lockup** | How the parts of the mark sit together. Symbol beside the name, stacked above it, or worked into the letters themselves. The size of the symbol against the name and which one leads. The space between the parts and how tight or open the whole sits. Where a tagline goes if there is one. Whether the parts read as one unit or as pieces placed near each other. |
| **Letterforms** | The letters themselves. Serif or sans, light or heavy, narrow or wide. Whether they are drawn custom for the mark or set in an existing font. Uppercase, lowercase, or a mix, and what that does to the tone. The ends of the strokes, cut flat or rounded off. How tight or open the spacing between letters is, and what that does to the presence of the name. |
| **Massing** | How heavy or light the mark sits. Drawn as an outline, filled solid, or a mix of both. How the empty space inside and around the mark gets used, and whether that space is doing design work of its own. The balance of thick and thin across the mark and whether it stays even. Whether the weight reads the same small as it does large. |

### Icon
*Symbolic representation of a thing or action*

| Control | What to Address in the Snippet |
|---|---|
| **Construction** | The shared rules the icon is drawn to. The grid it sits on and the visual size it holds inside its frame. Whether every icon in the set shares the same stroke weight, corner rounding, and level of detail. How strictly the rules hold when a shape fights the grid, kept optically even rather than mathematically exact. This is what makes a set read as one family rather than a collection of separate drawings. |
| **Edge** | Whether the corners are sharp or rounded and the ends of the strokes pointed or soft. How far the rounding goes, a slight ease on the corners or fully pill-shaped. Whether the edge treatment stays the same across every corner and end. This is the single biggest call for whether the icon feels technical and serious or friendly and approachable. |
| **Massing** | Outline, solid fill, or a mix of the two. How heavy or light the icon sits. The weight of the strokes and whether it stays even throughout. How the empty space inside is used, since a lot of what reads in an icon is the gaps. Whether the icon still reads clearly at a small size. |
| **Texture** | Whether the icon is flat and clean or carries a surface. Plain fills, or shadow, gradient, and grain layered on. How much depth the treatment suggests, a flat sticker or a small object with light falling on it. The flat modern look versus the older glossy, dimensional one, and which era the choice points to. |

### Subject/Illustration
*A drawn or rendered scene or object*

| Control | What to Address in the Snippet |
|---|---|
| **Perspective** | How space is drawn. Flat with no depth at all, straight on, angled with one or two vanishing points, or set at an isometric angle. Where the viewer stands in the space, looking up at it, down on it, or level with it. How correct or bent the perspective is, whether it follows the rules or stretches them on purpose. |
| **Shading** | How light and form get filled in. Flat with no shading at all, blocked in like a cartoon, smooth gradients, built up with hatching, or rendered to full volume. Where the light looks like it comes from and whether it stays consistent across the scene. How hard or soft the shadows fall. How much work the shading is doing, a hint of form or the whole sense of depth. |
| **Scene** | The world around the subject. Whether the subject sits in a full setting or floats on its own with nothing behind it. What fills the foreground, middle, and back, and how deep the space goes. How much detail the scene carries compared to the subject and where it thins out. What moment this appears to be, what just happened or is about to. Whether the scene is its own subject or just context to place the main one. |
| **Figures** | If there are people or creatures, how they are drawn. Their build and proportion, true to life or stretched and simplified. The pose they hold and what they are doing. How much detail faces and hands carry, since those are the parts most likely to go wrong in a generated image. Whether every figure is drawn in the same style or some get more care than others. |

### Texture
*A pattern or texture element meant for overlay or underlay*

| Control | What to Address in the Snippet |
|---|---|
| **Element** | What the pattern or texture is made of. A repeating unit for a pattern, dots, lines, a small shape, or a motif. A continuous material for a texture, paper, fabric, stone, grain, or noise. How literal the element is, a recognizable thing or pure surface. The scale of the element against the work it will sit on. |
| **Spread** | How the element lays across the field. Tiled straight, offset row to row, or scattered loose for a pattern. Even and continuous for a texture. Which way it points, whether the spread has a direction or sits still. How strict or loose the repeat is, and whether the viewer can spot where it repeats. Whether the edges have to tile seamlessly so the field can extend without a visible seam. |
| **Density** | How packed or sparse the field is. How much of the surface the element covers and how much air is left between. Whether the density stays even or builds up in places. How much of the work above or underneath shows through, since the texture is there to sit over or under something else. |

### Lettering
*A word or phrase drawn as a graphic*

| Control | What to Address in the Snippet |
|---|---|
| **Letterforms** | The style of the letters themselves. Serif or sans, script or block, light or heavy, narrow or wide. Whether the letters are drawn by hand for this piece or set in an existing font. Uppercase, lowercase, or a mix, and what that does to the tone. The ends of the strokes and whether the letters connect or stand apart. |
| **Arrangement** | How the words sit on the surface. A straight line, an arc, stacked lines, or letters worked into each other. Which word leads and how size differences make that clear. How tight the lines sit against each other and whether letters from one line reach into the next. The overall shape the whole block of words makes. |
| **Embellishment** | What is added on and around the letters. Swashes and flourishes growing out of the strokes, outlines, inlines, shadows, or nothing at all. How much the decoration is doing, a light finish or most of the look. Whether it stays inside the letters or spills into the space around them. |
