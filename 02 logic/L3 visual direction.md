# Composition - Direction

Take a confirmed editorial outline and turn it into the composition direction: look at the media, the references, the design language, the graphic and conceptual devices available, and make the actual calls about how each part of the composition works, what each channel carries, which specific images land where, what graphic devices give it texture and identity, and what the copy's job is in each slot, without writing it.

Use after the editorial outline has been confirmed and the job is to plan the actual composition before writing copy into it.

Trigger for making the concrete decisions that connect editorial intent to visual and structural reality. For looking at the real media candidates and placing them. For looking at the real references and adopting the structural, graphic, and visual language strategies they demonstrate. For identifying the through-line and the specific devices (micrographics, typographic events, data treatments, spatial moves, conceptual/metaphorical framing) that make the composition more than containers of content. For splitting the work across channels so the copy step knows exactly what it's responsible for and what it must leave alone.

What this prevents is copy written in a vacuum that doesn't know what's next to it, layouts that are just containers waiting for content, and compositions where every section does the same thing at the same density.

Do not use before the editorial outline exists and is confirmed. Do not write copy. The copy prompt follows this and writes into the plan this produces.

---

## Inputs you're working from

Not every composition arrives with the same set of inputs. The confirmed editorial outline is always present. The strategic brief is always present. The rest depends on how far the project has been developed. Read everything you're given before doing anything, and understand what role each module plays so you know what's available to make decisions with and where you're working without support.

**Confirmed editorial outline** (always present). The full ordered account of what the composition covers, the substance and purpose of each part, the single point, the feeling, and tensions. This is the editorial position. Every structural and visual decision you make here serves it.

**Strategic brief** (always present). Names the subject, coverage goals, design motifs or graphic ideas already committed to, and the types of imagery planned. The brief's commitments are constraints, not suggestions.

**Knowledge module** (when present). The raw source material. You may need to revisit it for specifics that the editorial outline summarized or for details that become relevant now that you're making placement decisions.

**Media module** (when present). This is the step where you actually look at the media. Every candidate image and video, with its context directives. You are placing these into specific slots and making calls about what each one does for the composition. Not abstractly noting "visual proof exists." Looking at what the image actually shows, what it carries, what mood and information it contributes, and where in the composition it belongs. When there is no media module, the composition is text-driven, type-driven, or device-driven. That's a fundamentally different kind of composition and the direction should reflect it.

**References module** (when present). This is the step where you actually look at the references. Every layout and composition reference, with its context directives. You are adopting specific structural approaches, graphic devices, spatial strategies, and visual language moves from these. Not noting them as inspiration. Deciding which ones apply, how, and where. When there is no references module, the structural and visual language decisions come from the identity module, the components, the brief's stated motifs, or your own judgment. Flag when you're working without reference support.

**Identity module** (when present). Brand guidelines: graphic rules, typography, color, visual language standards. When present, these govern every visual and structural decision. The type scale, the color palette, the grid behavior, the graphic vocabulary are set. When not present, the references and their directives carry that weight, and visual language decisions are proposals rather than applications of a system.

**Component module** (when present). Actual buildable HTML/CSS sections and slides. These are committed design decisions that already exist. When present, the composition's units should be planned in terms of what's buildable, not in the abstract. Existing components may already carry parts of the editorial outline's content. Account for what they cover and what they don't.

## The through-line

Before breaking the composition into units, name the through-line. This is the recurring idea that holds the whole composition together and gives it an identity beyond the content. It's the thing someone would point to and say "that's the concept" or "that's the move."

It might be graphic (a recurring visual element, a color treatment, a motif that evolves across sections), structural (a consistent spatial relationship, a grid behavior, a rhythm), typographic (a recurring word or phrase in large type, a numbering or labeling system), conceptual (a metaphor expressed visually, an organizing analogy), or a combination.

Where it comes from: the intersection of the editorial outline's single point and feeling with whatever the references demonstrate, the identity allows, or the brief names as a design motif. If the references show a specific device in their context directives (a micrographic system, a particular way of handling transitions, a conceptual framing strategy), and it serves the composition's point and feeling, this is where you adopt it and say how it manifests.

## Graphic and visual language devices

Name the specific devices that operate at the unit level and between units. These are the things that give the composition texture, layers, and craft. They come from the references, the identity, and the brief's stated motifs. Be specific. Not "we'll use micrographics." Which micrographics, doing what, drawn from which reference, serving which parts of the composition.

**Micrographics:** illustrative elements, diagrams, icons, symbols, notation, small graphic moments that carry information or texture.

**Data and spec presentation:** how numbers, dimensions, stats get shown. Typographic scale, graphic treatment, comparative devices. Numbers can be a design event, not just content.

**Typographic devices:** pull quotes, oversized callouts, caption systems, label treatments, typographic hierarchy used as a visual event. The type scale and system from the identity or references.

**Spatial devices:** white space, grid breaks, bleeds, overlaps, density shifts, the relationship between packed and open.

**Transitional devices:** how the composition moves from one part to the next. Hard cuts, gradual shifts, a graphic element that bridges, a spatial exhale.

**Conceptual and metaphorical devices:** any overarching visual metaphor, analogy, or thematic framing that gets expressed through the design rather than through words. This can be the most powerful layer and the one most likely to get lost if it's not named explicitly here.

If any of these are proposals rather than things already committed to in the brief, identity, or references, flag them as proposals.

## The units

Break the composition into its actual units, in order. What a unit is depends on the composition: sections of a scroll, screens, panels, slides, frames, zones within a single dense layout, whatever it actually is. The format is set by the brief, the components, or the references, not invented here. If none of those clearly set the format, flag that as an open decision and propose options with reasoning.

A page says things through several channels at once: photography and video, oversized headline type, body text, small functional text (captions, labels, buttons), graphic devices (numbers, diagrams, pull quotes, micrographics), and the through-line. The composition direction's job is to decide, for each unit, what each channel does and where the specific media and devices land.

Every unit gets:

**What it does.** Which part of the editorial outline it carries. Not a label. The actual substance and purpose, connected to the specific editorial outline content it draws from.

**The image.** Which specific media candidate or candidates from the media module. What the image actually shows and what it carries for this unit: place, mood, scale, texture, proof. If multiple candidates could work, say which and how the unit shifts under each. If no image, say so and say what fills that role. If the editorial outline flagged a visual proof gap here, this is where that gap becomes a concrete problem and you say how the unit works without it. If there is no media module, this entry describes what the unit's visual presence is instead: type, graphic device, spatial treatment, or nothing.

**What the image carries that the words don't need to.** This is the discipline. If the photo shows a vast, quiet desert landscape, the words don't say "vast and serene." The image already did that. Name specifically what the image takes off the copy's plate.

**The graphic devices in play.** Which of the devices named above appear in this unit and what they do. A micrographic element labeling something in the image. A large number as a typographic event. A diagrammatic element showing a relationship the photo can't. Be specific about what each device carries.

**What the copy's job is.** How much space the words get (if the type scale is set by the identity, components, or references, that governs). What the words are responsible for given what the image and graphic devices are already handling. The things only the words can carry: specifics, names, numbers, claims, promises, logic, voice, the ask, the thing the photo can't show.

**What the copy must not do.** For each unit, name what another channel is already carrying that the copy must leave alone. This is the most important line in each unit. It's the thing that prevents copy from restating the image, echoing the headline in the caption, explaining the concept the through-line already embodies.

**The feeling of this unit.** Not the whole composition's feeling. This unit's. A composition needs rhythm, which means not every section hits the same way. Some are open and quiet, some are dense and urgent, some are transitional exhales. Name what this one is.

## How to handle what's open

Some decisions are made: the editorial outline locked the sequence, the brief committed to certain motifs, the identity sets hard rules, certain media candidates are obvious fits.

Other things are still open. For anything open, present the options and say how the composition shifts under each. Say which you'd choose, in terms of what best serves the single point and the feeling. Leave the choice to the user.

The most consequential open decisions are usually: which image leads, whether a section is image-driven or type-driven, and whether a graphic device or a photograph carries a particular piece of the story. Those shift everything downstream, including what the copy has to do. Surface them clearly.

---

## What to output

**The through-line.** What it is, where it comes from, how it manifests across the composition.

**Graphic and visual language devices.** Every device in play, what it does, where it appears, where it comes from. Flagged as committed or proposed.

**The units, in order.** Each one with everything named above: what it does, the specific image, what the image carries, the graphic devices, the copy's job, what the copy must not do, and the unit's feeling.

**Open decisions.** Anything unresolved, with options, downstream implications, and a recommendation.

The next step takes this and writes the copy into it.
