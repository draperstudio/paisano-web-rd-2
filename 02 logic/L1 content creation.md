# Content Creation Framework

Pulled from Notion (R&D > Content Creation) on 7.11.26.

This document details our content creation framework and methodology, specifically how we decide what to make and how to make it, for any brand, any audience, any subject.

**Framework Pieces**

- **Goal** — why this piece exists
- **Move** — which person, from which stage to which
- **Person** — read across the planes
- **Want** — the live want you're pulling on
- **Message** — the one thing the piece puts in front of the person.
- **Angle** — how you enter and deliver it
- **Execution** — format first, then the actual words, images, design

**First Principles**

Brands make content to achieve a goal.

A brand has one big goal, and under it, smaller goals that serve it.

Single pieces of content don't carry the big goal alone, instead they carry one of the smaller goals, though always ultimately in support of the main goal.

You achieve any goal in content through the person on the other end of the content. The goal gets met if someone on the other end does, thinks, or feels something different than before.

That difference is a move from where the person was before the content to where they are after it.

A person is at one of a few stages with the brand: they don't know it exists, they know it but feel nothing, they know it and feel something, they trust it, and eventually they're ready to act—whatever that action means for our brand.

A single piece of content moves a person one stage forward, not several.

To move someone, you have to understand them first.

A person's reality sits across seven planes:

- **Experiential** — what they want their day-to-day life to feel like
- **Practical** — what they're trying to get done
- **Social** — how they want to be seen by other people
- **Emotional** — what they feel and what they want to feel
- **Values** — what they think is right and worth caring about
- **Identity** — how they see themselves
- **Fear & Risk** — what they're scared of and trying to avoid

Understanding someone isn't enough to move them. People don't change because you show them facts. They change based on how the mind works. There's a known set of drivers behind that, and they do two jobs here: they sharpen how you read the person across the planes, and they're the tools you use to build the piece.

The drivers split in two.

What the person already carries in and what they want. These overlap with the planes and sharpen your read of them. You find the one or two that are live and relevant to your goal and align the piece with them.

- **Status** — rank higher in the eyes of people whose regard counts
- **Belonging** — be inside a group and connected to its people
- **Autonomy** — act on choices that feel like their own
- **Competence** — be good at what you do and feel yourself getting better
- **Security** — certainty, predictability, not being wrong or exposed
- **Curiosity** — close the gap between what they know and what they sense is knowable
- **Identity** — keep acting in line with who they already are and who they said they'd be

What triggers the move. A mechanism works when it pulls on a want the person actually holds. These are the tools you use when you build the piece:

| Mechanism | Principle (how people work) | How you use it |
|---|---|---|
| Information gap | A specific open question nags at people until it's answered | Open a specific, answerable question and leave the answer missing |
| Loss aversion | A loss lands harder than a gain of the same size | Frame the move around what's lost by not acting, not only what's gained |
| Endowment | People value something more once it feels like theirs | Get them to hold, try, or picture the thing as already theirs |
| Scarcity | People want something more when there's less of it or it's going away | Surface a real limit on supply, time, or access |
| Social proof | People follow others like them, most when they're unsure | Show the right peers already doing it, in real numbers |
| Authority | People defer to expertise they find credible | Show earned credibility instead of asserting it |
| Liking | People are moved more by someone similar, warm, or on their side | Establish shared ground and candor before the ask |
| Reciprocity | People feel they owe you once you've given first | Give something genuinely useful first, no ask attached |
| Consistency | Once people take a small step, they keep acting to match it | Get a small, freely-made yes, then ask for the step that matches it |

**Angle — how it's framed for delivery**

- **Observational.** Present it as it happens with little framing, and let the person draw the conclusion.
- **Expository.** Explain a subject directly and lay it out plainly.
- **How-to.** Hand the person a method they can use themselves.
- **Demonstration.** Show the thing working so the person sees it do what it does.
- **Narrative.** Follow a person or events as they move over time.
- **Profile.** Hold still on one person or subject and let the portrait carry it.
- **Personal.** Speak from the maker's own experience and stake.
- **Investigative.** Uncover something not obvious and lay out what you found.
- **Comparison.** Set it against something else so the contrast carries the point.
- **Challenge.** Contest a common belief or the standard way of doing it.
- **Reactive.** Respond to something happening now.
- **BTS.** Show the real work that's normally unseen.
- **Archival.** Present existing or historical material and let the artifacts carry it.
- **Catalog.** Treat the subject as an entry in a record: named, classified, placed in a system.
- **Listicle.** Gather many things into a collected or ranked set.
- **Poetic.** Build feeling and impression through tone and imagery instead of explanation.
