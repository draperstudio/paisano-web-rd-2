# Chihuahuan Desert, Ranching, Food, and Restoration Craft
Covers the Chihuahuan Desert environment around Marfa, West Texas ranching, regional foodways and ingredients, and the craft of restoring an early-20th-century masonry building. It does not cover the hotel's own menu, which is not yet set.

## 1. THE CHIHUAHUAN DESERT

**Size and extent (sources conflict; all listed)**
- Largest desert in North America; covers nearly 250,000 sq mi (647,500 sq km); over 90% in Mexico; extends from south of Albuquerque, New Mexico, to about 250 km north of Mexico City (https://www.nps.gov/im/chdn/ecoregion.htm).
- Area ~193,783 sq mi (501,896 km²); described as the largest hot desert in North America (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chihuahuan_Desert).
- The Chihuahuan is the largest HOT desert in North America; the Great Basin Desert (a cold desert) is the largest desert in the U.S. overall (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_North_American_deserts).

**High-desert setting and climate**
- Higher elevation and more inland than the Sonoran Desert; mostly 480–1,800 m (1,575–5,906 ft); highest point Guadalupe Peak, 8,751 ft (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chihuahuan_Desert).
- Annual precipitation 150–500 mm (~6–20 in); summer monsoon rains predominate; winters cool to cold and dry; cooler and moister than the Sonoran and Mojave due to elevation (https://www.nps.gov/im/chdn/ecoregion.htm; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chihuahuan_Desert).
- Marfa is semi-arid (Köppen BSk); hot summers, cool winters; mean annual precipitation cited 14–20 in, mainly May–October, wettest in July (~2.2 in); 180–240 frost-free days (https://en.climate-data.org/north-america/united-states-of-america/texas/marfa-872367/; https://www.usclimatedata.com/climate/marfa/texas/united-states/ustx0830; https://soilseries.sc.egov.usda.gov/OSD_Docs/M/MARFA.html).

**Flora**
- As many as 3,500 plant species; nearly a quarter of the world's cactus species; about 1,000 species endemic to the ecoregion (https://www.nps.gov/im/chdn/ecoregion.htm).
- Lechuguilla (Agave lechuguilla) is endemic and an indicator species marking the desert's geographic limits (https://tpwd.texas.gov/landwater/land/programs/landscape-ecology/ems/emst/shrublands/chihuahuan-creosotebush-desert-scrub).
- Dominant shrub is creosote bush (Larrea tridentata); other conspicuous plants: soaptree yucca, desert prickly pear, sotol, and claret cup cactus (https://tpwd.texas.gov/landwater/land/programs/landscape-ecology/ems/emst/shrublands/chihuahuan-creosotebush-desert-scrub; https://www.nps.gov/im/chdn/ecoregion.htm).

**Fauna**
- More than 170 amphibian/reptile species, more than 130 mammal species (including pronghorn and mule deer), about 400 bird species, and about 110 fish species (https://www.nps.gov/im/chdn/ecoregion.htm).
- Also documented: kit fox, mountain lion, Montezuma quail, roadrunner, aplomado falcon, and rattlesnakes (https://www.oneearth.org/ecoregions/chihuahuan-desert/).

**The Marfa Plateau / grasslands**
- Marfa sits on a Chihuahuan Desert plateau in the Trans-Pecos, a "mile-high desert grassland" of basin-and-range topography between the Davis Mountains and the Chinati Mountains / Rio Grande (https://www.desertusa.com/cities/tx/marfa.html).
- Dominant grasses: blue grama, sideoats grama, black grama, tobosa, bear grass, and cane bluestem, with scattered mesquite; home to a large pronghorn herd, plus desert mule deer and scaled (blue) quail (https://www.desertusa.com/cities/tx/marfa.html).
- The Trans-Pecos is the largest U.S. portion of true Chihuahuan Desert (https://www.oneearth.org/ecoregions/chihuahuan-desert/).

## 2. WEST TEXAS / TRANS-PECOS RANCHING

**Early history**
- Milton Faver was Presidio County's first cattle baron; he moved to the Chinati Mountains area in 1857, bought land around three springs (Cibolo, Cienega, La Morita), built private forts, and raised both cattle and sheep (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/presidio-county).
- Lucas Brite left Frio County in spring 1885 with about 730 cattle; by the following year there were about 60,000 cattle in Jeff Davis County; some 1880s ranchers came fleeing a Texas fever epidemic elsewhere in the state (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/jeff-davis-county).

**Herefords**
- In 1884 rancher Jim Hiler brought the first Hereford to Presidio County; the breed replaced the longhorn and became known as the "Marfa Highland Hereford," supported by the northern plateau's grama grasses (https://www.texasmonthly.com/being-texan/marfa-the-last-frontier/; https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/presidio-county).

**Infrastructure**
- The 1882 railroad enabled livestock transport; W.F. Mitchell built the county's first barbed-wire fence at Antelope Springs in 1888; windmills, wells, and earthen tanks came in the late 1880s (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/presidio-county).

**Presidio County livestock census (cattle / sheep)**
- 1880: 2,496 / 9,030; 1900: 41,500 / 236; 1910: ~49,000 / 109; 1920: 37,500 / 5,312; 1930: 33,500 / 16,000; 1940: ~63,000 / <41,000 (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/presidio-county).

**Sotol**
- Sotol is a distilled spirit from Dasylirion (desert spoon), in the Asparagaceae family, not an agave. It grows across the Chihuahuan Desert of northern Mexico, New Mexico, Arizona, and west/central Texas (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sotol).
- Indigenous peoples of West Texas and northern Mexico used the sotol plant for centuries before European contact: for food, fiber, tools, and a drink of ceremonial significance; it was commercially distilled in El Paso in the late 19th century before Prohibition (https://authentictexas.com/sotol-the-original-distilled-beverage-of-texas/).

## 3. REGIONAL / CHIHUAHUAN-DESERT FOODWAYS AND INGREDIENTS

**Chiltepin (native chile)**
- The chiltepin is the official native pepper of Texas, designated when Gov. George W. Bush signed HCR No. 82 on June 18, 1997 (https://statesymbolsusa.org/symbol-official-item/texas/state-food-agriculture-symbol/chiltepin).
- It is the only wild chile pepper native to the United States, growing wild in canyons from West Texas through southern Arizona in the Sonoran and Chihuahuan Deserts; it is protected within Big Bend National Park (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiltepin; https://www.chilipeppermadness.com/chili-pepper-types/medium-hot-chili-peppers/chiltepin-chili-peppers/).

**Northern Mexican / border cuisine (relevant to the Trans-Pecos)**
- Northern Mexican cuisine relies heavily on beef and dairy, reflecting extensive cattle ranching, and produces Mexico's widest variety of cheeses, including queso fresco, queso menonita (Chihuahua), and asadero (https://www.foodrepublic.com/1296266/regional-cuisines-of-mexico/).
- Cabrito (roasted young goat), associated especially with Monterrey, is slow-roasted over mesquite coals (https://www.foodrepublic.com/1296266/regional-cuisines-of-mexico/).
- Documented wild desert food plants of the border region include nopales (prickly pear pads), tunas (prickly pear fruit), mesquite bean/flour, cholla buds, and tepary beans (https://borderlore.wordpress.com/tag/recipes/; https://tucsonfoodie.com/2019/08/30/sonoran-food-guide/).
- No source documents a formally codified cuisine literally named "Chihuahuan desert cooking"; the sourced basis is the border / Northern Mexican foodways above.

## 5. HISTORIC RESTORATION CRAFT (early-20th-century masonry building)

**Governing standards**
- The Secretary of the Interior's Standards for Rehabilitation are ten standards codified at 36 CFR Part 67, administered by the National Park Service; key principles include retaining historic character, avoiding false historical features, preserving distinctive features and craftsmanship, repairing rather than replacing where possible, using the gentlest cleaning methods (no sandblasting), and making new work compatible with yet differentiated from the old (https://www.nps.gov/subjects/taxincentives/secretarys-standards-rehabilitation.htm).

**Tax credits (context for restoring a National Register building)**
- A 20% federal income tax credit is available for rehabilitation of historic, income-producing buildings listed in or eligible for the National Register, with work certified by NPS as meeting the Secretary's Standards (https://www.nps.gov/subjects/taxincentives/tax-credit-basics.htm).
- Texas offers a 25% Texas Historic Preservation Tax Credit for rehabilitation of income-producing or nonprofit-use historic buildings; established by HB 500 (83rd Legislature), effective January 1, 2015; administered by the Texas Historical Commission with the Texas Comptroller; the property must be National Register-listed/eligible or a Recorded Texas Historic Landmark / State Antiquities Landmark; the credit is transferable (https://thc.texas.gov/preserve/grants-tax-credits-and-funding/historic-preservation-tax-credits/texas-historic; https://comptroller.texas.gov/taxes/historic-structures/).
- Program record January 2015–January 2025: roughly 1,100 initial applications, 496 completed/certified, $5.3 billion total investment (https://thc.texas.gov/sites/default/files/2025-04/tax_credit_report_2024.pdf).

**Masonry and mortar**
- NPS Preservation Brief 2, "Repointing Mortar Joints in Historic Masonry Buildings," advises addressing the root cause of deterioration before repointing and matching new mortar to the historic in composition, color, texture, and joint profile; it notes masonry cement and hydrated lime were introduced as new products in the 1930s (https://home1.nps.gov/tps/how-to-preserve/briefs/2-repoint-mortar-joints.htm).

**Plaster**
- NPS Preservation Brief 21, "Repairing Historic Flat Plaster: Walls and Ceilings," covers traditional wet-plaster repair with replacement options for severely deteriorated plaster (https://www.nps.gov/orgs/1739/preservation-briefs.htm).

**Signs and neon**
- NPS Preservation Brief 25, "The Preservation of Historic Signs," covers neon and warns against over-restoring so that evidence of age is not lost (https://home.nps.gov/tps/how-to-preserve/briefs/25-signs.htm).
- Documented neon restoration steps: condition assessment and documentation, gentle cleaning, hand glass-tube bending to match originals, and rewiring to modern electrical/safety standards while keeping the original look (https://frankbaltonsigns.com/neon-sign-restoration/; https://signmakers.com/restoring-vintage-neon-signs-bringing-history-back-to-life/).
