# The Building

Renovation items are labeled **[PLANNED]**. Sources are linked inline. Conflicts are listed in the final section.

Covers the Hotel Paisano building (historic name "El Paisano Hotel"), Marfa, Texas: its architect, architecture, landmark status, and ownership history. The building's role in the 1956 film *Giant* is in section 10.

**Address note:** No source supports "101 Highland Ave." The 1978 National Register nomination gives the location as the northwest corner of North Highland and West Texas Streets (https://atlas.thc.texas.gov/NR/pdfs/78002973/78002973.pdf). Current listings give 207 N Highland Ave, Marfa, TX 79843 (https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/).

## 1. THE ARCHITECT: HENRY TROST AND TROST & TROST

- Full name Henry Charles Trost; born March 5, 1860, in Toledo, Ohio; died September 19, 1933, in El Paso, Texas (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- His father immigrated from Germany in the early 1850s and worked as a carpenter and building contractor (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- Trost worked as an architectural draftsman in Toledo until 1880, then worked in Denver, Pueblo, Colorado Springs, Fort Worth, Galveston, New Orleans, and Dodge City between 1880 and 1888 (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- From 1888 to 1896 he designed ornamental metal in Chicago and was influenced by Louis Sullivan and Frank Lloyd Wright, and by the Mission Revival California Building at the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- He moved to Tucson in 1899 and to El Paso in 1903 (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- The firm Trost & Trost was formed with his brother Gustavus Adolphus Trost; nephew George Ernest Trost assisted; structural engineer Adolphus Gustavus Trost (Gustavus's twin) joined in 1908 (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- Firm founding year conflicts: TSHA implies 1903; a secondary source says 1904 (https://authentictexas.com/the-hotels-that-trost-built/).
- Trost & Trost designed at least 300 buildings in El Paso and hundreds more across West Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona; the brothers continued the firm until the early 1950s (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- Documented styles: Mission Revival, Pueblo Revival, Prairie School, Victorian, Art Deco, and reinforced-concrete construction; associated with the Chicago and Prairie Schools and the influence of Adler & Sullivan (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles; https://atlas.thc.texas.gov/NR/pdfs/78002973/78002973.pdf).
- Trost is credited with pioneering the use of steel-reinforced concrete (https://authentictexas.com/the-hotels-that-trost-built/).

**Other notable Trost buildings (dates where sourced):**
- Gage Hotel, Marathon, Texas: commissioned 1926 by Alfred Gage; two-story yellow brick, Mission/Spanish style; opening year given as both 1927 and 1928 across sources (https://gagehotel.com/history/; https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/gage-hotel/).
- Hotel El Capitan, Van Horn, Texas: 50 rooms; opened 1930; concrete built to resemble adobe (https://authentictexas.com/hotel-el-capitan/).
- Hotel Paso del Norte, El Paso: 1912 (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- The Paisano, El Capitan, the Gage, and the Paso del Norte are described as the firm's "four famous West Texas hotels" (https://authentictexas.com/the-hotels-that-trost-built/).
- Mills Building, El Paso (1911); El Paso High School (1916); San Angelo City Hall (1928); Driskill Hotel addition, Austin (1930); Bassett Tower, El Paso (1930); Trost residence, El Paso (1909) (https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/trost-henry-charles).
- The Holland Hotel in Alpine and Kerr Mercantile in Sanderson are also attributed to Trost's legacy (https://authentictexas.com/the-hotels-that-trost-built/).

## 2. THE BUILDING: DATES, DEVELOPER, CONSTRUCTION

All National Register (NRHP) facts from the 1978 nomination: https://atlas.thc.texas.gov/NR/pdfs/78002973/78002973.pdf

- Designed 1928; construction began June 1928; dedicated June 1930 (NRHP).
- Opening date conflicts among secondary sources: June 30, 1930 with a gala June 28, 1930 (https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/); June 1, 1930 (https://trostsociety.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/). NRHP says "Dedicated June 1930."
- Construction cost: $200,000 (https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel).
- Architect: Trost & Trost, El Paso. Contractor: Robert E. (R.E.) McKee, El Paso (NRHP; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel).
- Developer/owner at construction: Charles N. Bassett of El Paso / Southwestern Hotels, Inc. (Gateway Hotel Chain); financed through a community campaign and stock subscriptions (https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel).
- Built in anticipation of an oil boom that never materialized (NRHP).

**Structure, plan, and materials (NRHP):**
- Two-story commercial structure of reinforced concrete finished in stucco, painted white.
- "U" plan open to the south; a five-arch, one-story concrete screen connects the legs of the "U," enclosing a fifty-by-fifty-foot courtyard with the main entrance on axis.
- Exterior and interior load-bearing walls and floors are reinforced concrete.
- Interior partition walls are a combination of plastered pumice brick, concrete block, and hollow clay brick. (This differs from the internal Renovation Narrative's "steel lathe with concrete on both sides": see reconciliation section.)
- Three unconnected basements, all reinforced concrete.
- Original flat concrete roof covered in composition and tar; a swimming pool was added in the 1950s (NRHP continuation sheet).
- Approximate dimensions per secondary source: about 187 feet across by 112 feet deep (https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/).

**Room count history:**
- Originally 72 rooms on the first and second stories (NRHP; https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/).
- First-floor public rooms around the courtyard: lobby, lounge, public dining room, ball room, and coffee shop (NRHP).
- Post-2001 reopening counts reported inconsistently: 33 (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel), 41 (https://www.223collectionhotels.com/hotelpaisano/rooms/james-dean-room), and 42 (local reporting).

## 3. EXTERIOR AND INTERIOR FEATURES (NRHP)

- A low parapet on the roofline is ornamented with two courses of half-round orange roof tiles, creating the illusion of a tile roof.
- A double string-course runs between the first and second stories on the south, east, and courtyard facades.
- A three-foot terrazzo-tile "base" runs along the length of those same facades at ground level.
- East facade (fronting North Highland Street): five commercial shop bays on the first floor, split between the third and fourth bay by a large, ornate, two-story Spanish Baroque frontispiece doorway extending above the parapet; second floor has nine wooden double-hung eight-over-eight sash windows.
- Southeast corner: Spanish Baroque detailing with ball finials, pedimented window detailing, and a false wrought-iron balcony with brackets and drop pendants.
- Main entrance (within the courtyard, on axis): a two-story advance bay with a covered one-story concrete porch; restrained Spanish Baroque consoles, volutes, and ball finials cast in concrete.
- A small one-story addition at the southwest corner is now a restaurant.
- Style described variously as "quasi Spanish/Southwest," Spanish Baroque/Southwest Revival (NRHP), Mission/Spanish Revival (https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/), Spanish Colonial Revival (henrytrost.org registration data), and Spanish Revival (Trost Society).

## 7. NATIONAL REGISTER AND LANDMARK STATUS

- National Register of Historic Places reference number 78002973 (https://atlas.thc.texas.gov/NR/pdfs/78002973/78002973.pdf; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel).
- Received by NPS June 13, 1978; listed August 1, 1978 (Keeper's signature dated 7-31-78) (NRHP).
- Historic name on the form: "El Paisano Hotel," then called "El Paisano Inn" (1978).
- Recorded Texas Historic Landmark (RTHL) No. 1422, designated 1979 (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel).
- Nomination prepared by de Teel Patterson Tiller, Architectural Historian, West Texas Council of Governments, El Paso, dated April 18, 1978 (NRHP).
- Evaluated significance level: "Local" (NRHP).
- Legal description: Lots 25 through 32, Block 9, original city of Marfa; each lot 25 feet east–west by 125 feet north–south; bounded east by North Highland Street, south by West Texas Street, west and north by an alley (NRHP).
- The hotel sits within a block of the Presidio County Courthouse (NRHP).

## 8. OWNERSHIP AND OPERATION HISTORY

- 1970: Gary and Carolyn Rogers purchased the property (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel).
- Late 1970s: sold to Jack Brown of El Paisano Properties Corporation (Hobbs, New Mexico); 65 rooms were converted into 9 timeshare condominiums (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel; NRHP lists El Paisano Properties Corporation as owner at nomination).
- As of the 1978 nomination the building was being converted into condominiums under "sensitive restoration and adaptive reuse work" (NRHP).
- By around 2001 the building had been converted to timeshare units and had fallen into disrepair (https://theclio.com/entry/165520).
- March 2001: Joe and Lanna Duncan purchased the hotel at auction for $185,000 (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel).
- After about three years of renovation the hotel reopened; the Duncans are listed as owners from 2010 onward (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Paisano_Hotel; https://www.henrytrost.org/buildings/el-paisano-hotel/).
- (Current ownership by Hotel Paisano Partners LLC, closed July 1, 2026, is documented in the internal Project Context; "Joe and Lana" in the internal docs are the Duncans, the prior owners.)

## 10. THE FILM GIANT (1956)

The building opened in 1930. Giant was filmed in Marfa in 1955.

- Directed by George Stevens, produced by Warner Bros., based on Edna Ferber's novel; filmed in the Marfa / Presidio County area in the summer of 1955 (a roughly six-week shoot) (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_(1956_film); NRHP).
- Other filming locations: Albemarle County, Virginia (opening) and Warner Bros. Burbank studios (interiors) (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_(1956_film)).
- The El Paisano Hotel served as the base of operations; Stevens and 300-plus cast and crew stayed there, including James Dean, Elizabeth Taylor, Rock Hudson, Sal Mineo, Chill Wills, and Jane Withers (NRHP).
- Full principal cast: Elizabeth Taylor, Rock Hudson, James Dean, Carroll Baker, Jane Withers, Chill Wills, Mercedes McCambridge, Dennis Hopper, Sal Mineo, Rod Taylor, Elsa Cárdenas, Earl Holliman (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_(1956_film)).
- Stevens used the hotel ballroom to screen dailies; the hotel does not appear in the film itself and functioned as the off-set social hub (https://gov.texas.gov/film/trail/hotel-paisano).
- Rock Hudson Suite: three rooms combined; the largest suite in the hotel; includes the room Rock Hudson occupied; located directly across from Elizabeth Taylor's room (https://stephaniedrenka.com/rock-hudson-suite-hotel-paisano/; https://www.223collectionhotels.com/hotelpaisano/rooms/james-dean-room).
- James Dean Room: the room James Dean stayed in while playing Jett Rink; one of the smaller original historic rooms; one queen bed; originally tiled European bath (shower only); French doors to a north-side balcony with a view toward the Presidio County Courthouse (https://www.223collectionhotels.com/hotelpaisano/rooms/james-dean-room).
- Elizabeth Taylor's room is also bookable; the three rooms of Hudson, Taylor, and Dean are available (https://stephaniedrenka.com/rock-hudson-suite-hotel-paisano/).
- The hotel maintains a Giant memorabilia room with photographs, movie posters, and memorabilia (https://gov.texas.gov/film/trail/hotel-paisano; https://bigbendtimes.com/2023/04/08/hotel-paisano-a-nostalgic-journey-through-the-giant-memorabilia-room/).
- The Reata mansion set was built on the Ryan Ranch (also referenced as Evans Ranch), 17 miles west of Marfa; the façade was built in Burbank for $100,000, measured 83 feet across with a tower reaching 64 feet, was shipped to Marfa by train on six flat cars, and was erected as a three-sided structure on six telephone poles; it stood until it crumbled in the 1980s (https://giggster.com/guide/movie-location/where-was-giant-filmed).
- Release: New York premiere October 10, 1956; nationwide U.S. release November 24, 1956 (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_(1956_film)).
- James Dean was killed in an automobile accident on September 30, 1955, during editing and before release; Nick Adams dubbed some of Dean's dialogue (https://giggster.com/guide/movie-location/where-was-giant-filmed; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_(1956_film)).
- *Giant* was Dean's last of three leading roles and earned him a posthumous Best Actor nomination; at the 29th Academy Awards George Stevens won Best Director, and the film was nominated for Best Picture, Best Actor (Dean and Hudson), Best Supporting Actress (McCambridge), and others (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_(1956_film)).

## 11. CONFLICTS AND RECONCILIATION WITH INTERNAL DOCUMENTS

- **Interior wall construction.** The internal Renovation Narrative states interior walls are "steel lathe with concrete on both sides." The primary NRHP nomination states interior partition walls are plastered pumice brick, concrete block, and hollow clay brick, while exterior/load-bearing walls and floors are reinforced concrete. These disagree; the renovation team's on-site observation and the 1978 document should be reconciled before either is treated as fact.
- **Courtyard dimensions.** NRHP (primary, original) gives 50 by 50 feet. Internal strategy documents give 45 by 45 feet. Unresolved.
- **Opening date.** "Dedicated June 1930" (NRHP) vs June 30, 1930 vs June 1, 1930 (secondary sources).
- **Firm founding year.** 1903 (TSHA) vs 1904 (Authentic Texas).
- **Post-restoration room count.** 33 vs 41 vs 42 across sources; the internal count is ~40–42 now, moving to 48 after reconfiguration **[PLANNED]**.
- **Address.** "101 Highland Ave" unsupported; NRHP corner description and the modern 207 N Highland Ave should be confirmed.
