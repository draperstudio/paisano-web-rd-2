## RENOVATION: CONSTRUCTION APPROACH (ALL [PLANNED])

- Every room gets the same base renovation regardless of class: new flooring, painted v-groove panel on the ceiling and walls, and a bathroom refresh with new toilet, new plumbing fixtures, new sconce, and grout cleaning. [RN]
- The v-groove panels go directly over the existing surfaces with no demolition required. [RN]
- The rooms where the vending closet and housekeeping area are being converted have bare concrete walls now; the v-groove goes over that too. [RN]
- Interior walls throughout the building are steel lathe with concrete on both sides, including non-load-bearing walls. [RN]
- Demolition is difficult and expensive because of this wall construction (per prior owner Joe). The upside is sound isolation between rooms. [RN]
- Cutting a new door opening, filling an existing door, or adding/removing a wall is driven up in cost and complexity by the concrete construction. [RN]
- Interior doors need to be thicker than standard to match the frames. Estimate of around $700 per door, based on comparison to doors from Alpine Lumber for a Colorado project that ran $400–500 but would be too thin for these walls. A $2,800 custom door was discussed and dismissed as excessive. [RN]

## BATHROOM STRATEGY ([PLANNED])

- The base package keeps the existing historic tile, cleans the grout, and swaps in a new toilet, fixtures, and sconce. This covers roughly half the rooms. [RN]
- Beyond the base package, bathroom work falls into tiers: full retiling without moving anything; gut renovations where plumbing is moved; bathroom expansions that take space from adjacent areas (old kitchens, hallways, closets, neighboring rooms); and entirely new bathrooms built in spaces that were previously kitchens, offices, bedrooms, or nothing. [RN]
- One room currently has no bathroom at all. [RN]
- A few rooms on the east side of the upper floor get a smaller addition: large closets converted into small toilet rooms. [RN]
- Several bathroom expansions create dependencies between rooms; one room gives up space so another can have a bigger bathroom, or two rooms share a wall where one side expands into the other. [RN]
- One shared-wall expansion is uncertain because of the concrete wall thickness and whether it is feasible to break through. [RN]

## FURNITURE AND FF&E ([PLANNED])

- Standard furniture package per room: king bed, two nightstands, two lamps, a standard rug, a mini fridge, and two wall hooks. [RN]
- Lamps are used instead of sconces because the concrete walls make hanging things impractical. [RN]
- One junior suite keeps two queen beds with a dresser between them instead of nightstands. [RN]
- No room is to have a single queen. A queen is treated as a one-person bed in the current market; the Pearl gets complaints about single queens, and women traveling together will share a king but not a queen. [RN]
- Suites and larger rooms get additional furniture: couches (L-sectionals or standard couches with chairs), coffee tables, wet bars, and in some cases TV credenzas. Suite furniture does not follow a rigid standard because layouts vary. [RN]
- Some rooms need custom pieces: fitted corner closets designed as freestanding furniture, built-in banquettes. [RN]
- Rugs go on top of flooring in every room; suites with separate living areas get additional rugs. [RN]
- A minibar with sensors is planned for every room; the sensor type tracks what guests take. In smaller rooms the minibar lives in the closet; in larger rooms it can go inside a credenza or buffet piece the TV sits on. [RN]
- Minibar exact stocked inventory: NOT FOUND. See KNOWLEDGE GAPS.
- The TV question is unresolved. **[UNDECIDED]** The one-bedroom suites would have TVs at minimum. Streaming setup (ethernet and WiFi, guests log into their own accounts). Original historic rooms currently have wall-mounted TVs. Smaller rooms would stay wall-mounted if TVs go in. [RN]
- Standard-room furniture package estimated informally at around $2,000–2,500. [RN]
- There was mention of possibly doing the AC as part of the renovation. **[UNDECIDED]** [RN]

## SCOPE DISTRIBUTION ([PLANNED])

- The simplest rooms are the mezzanine patios: identical, base renovation plus patio cleanup and patio furniture. Fireplaces on the patios are assumed working but not confirmed. [RN]
- Mezzanine sun rooms are similar, with glassed-in sun rooms needing freshen-up and furniture instead of patios. [RN]
- The heaviest scope is concentrated in about a dozen rooms on the second floor, mostly the one-bedroom suites and some junior suites: kitchen conversions, multiple new walls and doors, wet bars, full living room furniture packages, and some entirely new bathroom builds. [RN]
- The Rock Hudson Suite is the biggest single project: brick archway removal, wet bar renovation, potential kitchen redo, full new deluxe bathroom with freestanding tub and outdoor shower, and a large exterior terrace needing new furniture and plantings. [RN]
- The original historic rooms and most deluxe historics are straightforward: base renovation and furniture with minor additions like a new door, a small wet bar, or a Juliet balcony removal. A handful get gut bathrooms, closet additions, or new hallway/vestibule construction. [RN]

## RATES AND REVENUE

- Current online rates: standard rooms $170 to $190 per night on weeknights during off-peak periods, above $200 on weekends. [RN]
- Suites list at $230 to $250 midweek. [RN]
- The Rock Hudson Suite was listed at $345 per night off-peak. [RN]
- All rates exclude the 13 percent tax (6 percent state plus 7 percent city). [RN]
- Rates are lowest in January and May and peak in October during Chinati Weekend, running roughly 40 percent higher than low-season rates. [RN]
- Saturday is consistently the most expensive night. [RN]
- The hotel does not charge a resort fee. [RN]
- There is a $40 per night pet fee. [RN] [STRAT]
- Post-renovation target rate is around $425 a night. **[PLANNED]** [STRAT]
- Buck built a room revenue estimate room by room, assigning rate categories by type and multiplying by 250 nights per year, producing $4,836,000 in total room revenue, described as hopeful but realistic in year three, not best case. **[PLANNED / PROJECTION]** [RN]
- Best case would be higher rates at around 70 percent occupancy; $4.85 million is the high case number for rooms. **[PROJECTION]** [RN]
